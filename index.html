📚 How It Works

Understanding Recommendation Systems in E-Commerce

01

Why does this platform use recommendation systems?

E-commerce platforms like Amazon use recommendation systems to:

  • Enhance User Experience — Help users discover relevant products from millions of listings without manual searching
  • Increase Revenue — Personalized recommendations drive 35% of Amazon's total sales revenue
  • Improve Engagement — Users spend more time on platforms that understand their preferences
  • Reduce Choice Overload — With 350M+ products, filtering is essential to prevent decision paralysis
  • Customer Retention — Satisfying recommendation experiences build brand loyalty
02

What type of data does it collect?

Shopping platforms collect multiple data types:

  • Explicit Data — Ratings, reviews, wishlists, purchase history
  • Implicit Data — Browsing history, click patterns, time spent on pages, search queries
  • Product Metadata — Name, category, brand, price, specifications, images
  • User Demographics — Age, location, device type, language preferences
  • Contextual Data — Time of day, season, trending events, inventory levels

Our system uses: Product names, categories, ratings, number of ratings, and pricing data

03

How does the recommendation system work?

Our system implements a multi-layered recommendation pipeline:

1 Data Ingestion & Cleaning
→
2 Feature Extraction (TF-IDF)
→
3 Similarity Computation
→
4 Score Aggregation
→
5 Ranked Results

The pipeline processes product metadata, builds feature vectors, computes similarity matrices, and combines multiple scoring signals for final ranking.

04

What techniques/algorithms are used?

🔗 Content-Based Filtering

Uses TF-IDF (Term Frequency-Inverse Document Frequency) to convert product text into numerical vectors, then computes Cosine Similarity to find products with similar descriptions.

similarity = cos(θ) = (A·B) / (||A|| × ||B||)

🔥 Popularity-Based

Uses Bayesian Weighted Rating (IMDB formula) to rank products. Accounts for both rating value and volume, preventing products with few but perfect ratings from dominating.

WR = (v/(v+m))×R + (m/(v+m))×C

💰 Value Scoring

Combines normalized discount percentage with normalized rating to find products offering the best value-for-money. Weights: 40% discount, 60% rating.

value = 0.4×norm_discount + 0.6×norm_rating

⚡ Hybrid Algorithm

Combines all three signals with configurable weights — 40% content similarity, 35% popularity, 25% value score — to produce the most balanced recommendations.

hybrid = 0.4×content + 0.35×pop + 0.25×value
05

How does it personalize content?

  • Product Similarity — When a user views/selects a product, similar items are surfaced using cosine similarity on TF-IDF vectors
  • Category Affinity — Filtering recommendations by the user's preferred product category
  • Budget Matching — Price-range filtering ensures recommendations fit the user's spending capacity
  • Brand Clustering — Products from similar brands are weighted higher in content-based results
  • Social Proof — Popularity scores leverage crowd wisdom (high ratings + high volume)
06

What challenges does it face?

  • Cold Start Problem — New products/users with no interaction history cannot be effectively recommended
  • Data Sparsity — Most users interact with a tiny fraction of available products
  • Scalability — Computing similarity matrices grows quadratically with catalog size
  • Filter Bubble — Over-personalization may trap users in echo chambers of similar products
  • Dynamic Inventory — Products go in/out of stock, prices change, new products arrive constantly
  • Bias & Fairness — Popular brands may receive disproportionate visibility
